WHAT IS DIWALI

Diwali, also known as Deepavali, is one of the most widely celebrated festivals in India and by Indian communities around the world. It is often referred to as the "Festival of Lights." Diwali is a multi-day Hindu festival that typically lasts five days and usually falls between October and November, depending on the lunar calendar.

The festival carries various cultural and religious significance, and it symbolizes the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. Diwali is celebrated with great enthusiasm and involves a range of customs and traditions, which can vary by region and community. Some common elements of Diwali celebrations include:

Some common elements of Diwali celebrations include:

Lighting Diyas and Candles:

The most iconic aspect of Diwali is the lighting of oil lamps or diyas and candles. This symbolizes the triumph of light over darkness and the dispelling of ignorance.

Decorations:

Homes are often decorated with colorful rangoli patterns, lanterns, and colorful lights. People clean and decorate their houses to welcome the goddess of wealth, Lakshmi.

Fireworks:

Fireworks displays are a major part of Diwali celebrations. People light fireworks to mark the occasion, creating a spectacular visual display.

Puja (Prayers):

Families often gather for special prayers and pujas during Diwali. They seek the blessings of deities, particularly the goddess Lakshmi, for wealth and prosperity.

Exchanging Gifts:

The exchange of gifts and sweets is a common Diwali tradition. It's a way of sharing joy and strengthening social bonds.

Feasting:

Special meals and sweets are prepared and shared among family and friends. Various traditional dishes are enjoyed during the festival.

New Clothes:

It is customary to wear new clothes on Diwali as a symbol of new beginnings and a fresh start.

Charity:

Many people also perform acts of charity during Diwali to help those in need, following the spirit of giving and sharing.

FIVE DAYS OF DIWALI

Dhanteras:

This marks the first day of Diwali. It is the day for worshiping Lord Dhanvantari, the physician of the gods, and seeking blessings for good health. People also perform a special puja for wealth and prosperity on this day. It is common to buy gold, silver, and utensils on Dhanteras as a symbol of good luck.

Naraka Chaturdashi or Choti Diwali:

This day commemorates the victory of Lord Krishna over the demon Narakasura. People take oil baths before sunrise, light lamps, and burst firecrackers to symbolize the victory of light over darkness.

Diwali:

The main day of the Diwali festival, it is celebrated with great enthusiasm. People clean and decorate their homes, light diyas and candles, offer prayers to Goddess Lakshmi, and exchange gifts and sweets with family and friends. Fireworks displays are a significant part of the celebrations.

Govardhan Puja and Annakut:

In some parts of India, the fourth day is dedicated to worshiping Lord Krishna and his lifting of the Govardhan Hill to protect the people of Vrindavan from torrential rain. Devotees prepare large feasts and offer a variety of food items to deities in a ritual called Annakut, which means "mountain of food".

Bhai Dooj

The fifth day of Diwali is dedicated to the bond between brothers and sisters. Sisters perform aarti for their brothers and apply a tilak (a mark) on their foreheads. Brothers, in turn, offer gifts to their sisters and promise to protect and take care of them. It is a day that celebrates the love and affection between siblings.
